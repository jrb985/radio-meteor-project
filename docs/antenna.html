DIY Antenna Options

Improving reception for 104.3 MHz FM meteor forward-scatter | Seattle

  • Goal: hear a distant FM carrier (Boise, ~650 km SE) reflected off meteor trails. That is a WEAK-signal task -- antenna and location matter a lot.
  • Bottom line up front: getting OUTSIDE and away from house noise is usually a bigger win than which antenna you build.
Slide 2 of 10

The RF you're working with

  • Frequency 104.3 MHz -> wavelength (lambda) ~ 2.88 m. Key sizes:
    • quarter-wave ~ 72 cm; half-wave ~ 144 cm (wire ~5% shorter).
  • What 'a better antenna' buys you:
    • GAIN on the weak distant path (more pings, fainter pings detected), DIRECTIVITY: aim at Boise (~SE) and reject strong LOCAL Seattle FM, LOWER NOISE: away from house electronics = lower noise floor.
  • Polarization: US FM is usually circular/mixed, so H or V both work; prefer HORIZONTAL to cut man-made (often vertical) noise.
  • Geometry: meteor echoes arrive from LOW elevation toward the transmitter, so aim ~SE, near the horizon (not straight up).

Your current whip: what to expect -- and is it worth it?

  • Worth it? YES, as a baseline / proof -- you already saw it receive local FM.
  • For WEAK meteor scatter, a small indoor whip is marginal:
    • expect low counts and only the STRONGER pings; many will be missed.
  • Quick wins with what you have (no build):
    • If it is the telescopic dipole kit, set each element to ~68-72 cm (1/4-wave).
    • Stand it VERTICAL as a whip, or better, lay the two elements HORIZONTAL in a line for a 1/2-wave dipole.
    • Move it to a window facing SE (toward Boise); get it as high as you can.
  • Use the whip to establish a baseline ping rate, then compare after upgrades.

Indoor vs outdoor -- the biggest lever

  • This likely matters MORE than antenna type.
  • Indoors you fight TWO problems:
    • Building loss: walls/roof/low-E windows/foil insulation = ~10-20 dB down.
    • Indoor noise: chargers, LED drivers, PCs, routers raise the VHF noise floor.
  • Outdoors, up, and away from electronics can gain ~10-20 dB of SNR -- often the single biggest improvement you can make.
  • Progression to try (each step usually helps):
    • SE-facing window -> attic -> outside on a short mast / eave.
  • Keep the antenna a few feet from metal gutters, wiring, and the house wall.

Option A: Half-wave dipole (recommended first build)

  • Simplest real upgrade. Two wires/rods, each ~68-69 cm, in a straight line (total ~1.37 m), fed at the center.
  • Materials: wire or copper tube, coax (RG-6 is cheap & low-loss), and a common-mode choke/balun at the feedpoint (a few ferrite turns of coax).
  • Orientation: HORIZONTAL, wire running NE<->SW so the broadside faces SE (dipole is bidirectional: SE and NW lobes; the NW lobe is just unused).
  • Pros: cheap, easy, big step up from a small whip, low noise if outside.
  • Cons: no front/back rejection of local FM (a beam does that).
Slide 7 of 10

Option B: Directional -- Moxon or 3-element Yagi (best SNR)

  • Adds gain AND rejects strong local Seattle FM coming from other directions -> cleaner detection of the weak Boise path.
  • Moxon (2-el): compact rectangle of wire on spreaders; ~5-6 dBi with good front-to-back; forgiving to build. Great value.
  • Yagi (3-el): reflector + driven + director on a ~1 m boom; ~7-8 dBi.
    • Elements ~1.3-1.5 m; use a calculator (e.g. for 104.3 MHz).
  • Aim the FRONT toward Boise (~SE, azimuth ~120-130 deg), low elevation.
  • Mount horizontally for H-pol; needs a small mast and a way to point it.
  • Cons: bigger, must be aimed; overkill until you're outside with low noise.

Feedline & noise tips (cheap dB you shouldn't skip)

  • Coax: RG-6 (75 ohm TV coax) is cheap and low-loss at FM; RTL-SDR is happy with it. Keep runs as short as practical.
  • Add a common-mode CHOKE at the feedpoint (coil the coax through a ferrite):
    • stops the coax shield from acting as an antenna and dragging house noise in.
  • Route coax away from power wiring, chargers, Ethernet, and the PC.
  • Strong local FM can OVERLOAD the RTL-SDR (desense): a directional antenna aimed at Boise + your fixed gain setting both help; back off gain if needed.
  • LNA / bias-tee: helps only if you're COAX-loss-limited, not NOISE-limited.
    • At FM with short coax you're usually noise-limited -> an LNA won't help much.

Recommended path (cheap -> serious)

  • Step 1 (free): tune the whip/dipole to ~68-72 cm elements, horizontal, at an SE window as high as possible. Baseline the ping rate.
  • Step 2 ($5-15): build a horizontal half-wave dipole and put it OUTSIDE, broadside to SE, with a feedpoint choke. Expect the biggest jump here.
  • Step 3 ($15-40): Moxon or 3-el Yagi aimed at Boise for real counts and rejection of local FM -- worth it once you're outside and noise-limited.
  • Re-baseline after each step (same run length) to measure the improvement.

Summary

  • The current whip is fine to PROVE the system and baseline, but expect low meteor counts, especially indoors.
  • Biggest single win: get the antenna OUTSIDE, up, and away from house noise.
  • Best cheap build: a horizontal half-wave dipole (~68 cm legs), broadside SE.
  • Best performance: a Moxon or 3-el Yagi aimed SE at Boise, once outdoors.
  • Feedline hygiene (RG-6 + feedpoint choke, routed away from noise) is free dB.
  • Measure each change with an equal-length run so you know what actually helped.
