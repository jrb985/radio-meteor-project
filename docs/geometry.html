Reflection Geometry & the Science of the Count

Why only certain meteors bounce a distant FM station into your antenna

  • Forward-scatter: a distant FM station, normally below your horizon, is relayed to you for an instant by a meteor's ionized trail. Each confirmed relay = 1 count.
  • The geometry decides WHICH meteors you see, WHERE the echo comes from, and HOW that shapes your diurnal & shower curves and your antenna aim.
Slide 2 of 11

The three players

  • TX -- a distant FM transmitter (KAWO Boise, 104.3 MHz, ~650 km), normally BELOW your radio horizon -> that channel is empty noise most of the time.
  • RX -- your antenna/RTL-SDR watching the empty channel.
  • The meteor trail -- a line of ionized plasma (free electrons) at ~85-120 km, created as the meteoroid ablates. It can briefly reflect VHF radio.
  • When the geometry is right, the trail relays TX -> RX and the channel lights up for a fraction of a second: a 'ping' (candidate count).

The specular / ellipsoid condition

A trail reflects to you only where it is TANGENT to a TX/RX-focus ellipsoid

  • meteor trail (tangent here)
  • P = reflection point (~100 km up)
  • TX (Boise, 104.3 MHz)
  • RX (your antenna)
  • Ellipsoid = all points P where TX->P->RX path length is constant (TX & RX are its foci).
  • At the tangent point the two ray angles are equal (ellipse reflection property) = specular condition.
Slide 4 of 11

Why only certain meteors reflect to YOU

  • Two conditions must BOTH hold:
    • PLACE -- the trail crosses ~85-120 km over the common volume, AND ORIENTATION -- it is tangent to the ellipsoid there (perpendicular to the bisector of the TX-P-RX angle).
  • A trail pointing the wrong way has no tangent point in your illuminated region.
  • -> You detect a SUBSET of meteors, never all of them. Undercounting is inherent.
  • A trail points AWAY from its radiant, so the RADIANT position controls which meteors can be specular for your particular TX-RX path.

Hot spots: where the echoes actually come from

  • Because trail orientation is fixed by the radiant, the specular solutions cluster in specific PATCHES off to the SIDES of the TX-RX great-circle path.
  • Echoes generally do NOT come from the midpoint straight down the Boise line -- they come from those side 'hot spots'.
  • Shower (single radiant): usually TWO hot-spot regions.
  • Sporadics (all radiants): the response smears into a broad sensitivity pattern.
  • The hot spots sit ~100 km up and a few hundred km away -> low elevation (next slide).

From geometry to antenna aim

  • Reflection point: ~100 km altitude, a few hundred km distant.
    • -> the echo arrives at a LOW elevation angle (~5-20 deg), near the horizon.
  • Direction: toward the path / transmitter -- roughly SE toward Boise.
  • So aim antenna gain SE and LOW, not at the zenith:
    • horizontal dipole broadside SE, or a Yagi/Moxon pointed at Boise.
  • Max usable TX-RX range ~2000 km (limited by the ~100 km reflection height and the horizon at both ends). 650 km (Boise) sits comfortably inside that.

Why this shapes the diurnal curve

  • Sporadic meteor rate peaks ~06h local: Earth's APEX (its direction of motion)
    • rides highest before dawn -> more meteors, more with usable geometry.
  • What you RECORD = the true meteor diurnal CONVOLVED with your fixed geometry + antenna pattern (which radiants have hot spots for you, and when).
  • So your radio curve still shows a clear daily rhythm, but its exact shape is a fingerprint of YOUR station -- keep the setup constant so it stays comparable.

Why shower profiles have structure

  • A shower has ONE radiant that drifts across the sky through the night.
  • Specular hot spots exist only for certain radiant positions/elevations, so the observed radio rate rises and falls with the radiant's geometry.
  • This can produce DOUBLE peaks or gaps that are NOT in the true meteor flux -- artifacts of the forward-scatter geometry, not the shower itself.
  • Interpreting activity therefore needs the IMO statistical-reduction method, not just raw counts (see the Science deck / references).

What you actually see: two echo types

  • UNDERDENSE trail (low electron density):
    • wave passes through; electrons scatter independently; brief spike with fast exponential decay (<~1 s). The majority of events.
  • OVERDENSE trail (high electron density):
    • reflects like a metal cylinder; longer echo (seconds), can flutter/distort as upper-atmosphere winds move the trail.
  • Both are real counts; the DURATION distribution carries meteoroid mass-index info.
Slide 10 of 11

From geometry to a 'count' (honest limits)

  • Each favorable meteor -> one candidate ping. But aircraft, sporadic-E, lightning, and interference also make bursts -> you must CLASSIFY (meteor vs the rest).
  • What you count = a RELATIVE, geometry-weighted meteor rate from a CONSISTENT setup -- not absolute flux.
  • One modest station CANNOT give absolute flux, velocity, radiant, or orbit.
  • Consistency is everything: same frequency, gain, antenna, threshold -> valid day-to-day and shower-to-shower comparisons.

Summary

  • Reflection needs the trail TANGENT to a TX/RX-focus ellipsoid (specular) -- only certain positions AND orientations qualify, so you sample a subset.
  • Echoes come from side 'hot spots' ~100 km up at low elevation -> aim antenna SE, low.
  • Apex motion + your geometry + antenna pattern together shape the diurnal & shower curves.
  • A 'count' = a classified meteor echo: relative, not absolute.
  • Filtering (classifier) + a constant setup are what make the counts scientifically meaningful.
