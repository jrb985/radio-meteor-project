Science from a Modest Radio Meteor Station

What's measurable, what's meaningful, and what an amateur can publish

  • Single RTL-SDR forward-scatter station, 104.3 MHz FM, Seattle.
  • Honest take: you won't get orbits from one station -- but consistent COUNTS, activity PROFILES, and echo statistics are real, contributable science.
Slide 2 of 11

What a single forward-scatter station measures

  • Directly observable per meteor 'ping':
    • time of occurrence, echo DURATION, peak amplitude/SNR, and (with care)
    • Doppler/shape from the snapshot -> under- vs over-dense classification.
  • Aggregated over time:
    • counts per hour/day; activity vs a shower's timeline; diurnal rhythm.
  • NOT obtainable from one modest station:
    • orbits, radiant, absolute velocity, true absolute flux (need multi-station / interferometry / calibrated geometry).
  • Caveat (per IMO): raw amateur counts are a POOR proxy for true flux -- they are modulated by setup & radiant geometry. Consistency + reduction help.

Meaningful product #1: counts & shower activity profiles

  • Count pings per hour continuously; overlay on a shower's expected timeline.
  • You can measure:
    • the RISE / PEAK / FALL of a shower and WHEN your local peak occurs, relative activity night-to-night and shower-vs-sporadic contrast.
  • Contribute the monthly counts to RMOB (global aggregation since 1993) and compare with other stations -- this is the bread-and-butter amateur product.
  • Highest value: DAYTIME showers (e.g. Arietids, ~Jun 7) that ONLY radio can observe -- a genuine niche where amateur radio data matters.
Slide 4 of 11

Meaningful product #2: diurnal variation & sporadic background

  • The sporadic meteor rate rises and falls each day (max ~06h local, min ~18h)
    • as Earth's leading 'apex' side sweeps up more meteoroids.
  • A week of continuous data reproduces this classic sinusoid -- a satisfying, verifiable first result that confirms known physics with YOUR hardware.
  • Also visible: seasonal changes in sporadic background; day-to-day weather of the near-Earth meteoroid environment.
  • Great teaching / write-up material even before any shower.
Slide 5 of 11

Meaningful product #3: echo-duration statistics

  • Separate UNDERDENSE (brief, exponential-decay) from OVERDENSE (longer, can last seconds) echoes -- your snapshots already show the difference.
  • The DURATION DISTRIBUTION (cumulative log-log of duration vs count) carries information about the meteoroid MASS INDEX of the heavier population.
  • Overdense echo duration also has a diurnal signature (linked to upper-atmos chemistry/ozone) -- see the NASA paper in references/.
  • Needs decent counts (-> better antenna) but is a real analysis, not just a tally.

Where amateurs actually publish / contribute

  • RMOB (Radio Meteor Observing Bulletin): submit monthly counts; appears in the monthly bulletin -- the standard amateur radio-meteor output.
  • eMeteorNews: amateur-friendly journal; publishes radio observation reports and shower analyses (a realistic venue for a written-up result).
  • IMO / WGN (Journal of the IMO): more formal analyses; collaborative campaigns.
  • BRAMS Radio Meteor Zoo (Zooniverse): citizen-science classification of spectra.
  • Realistic bar: solo peer-reviewed papers are rare; RMOB + eMeteorNews + campaign contributions + catching an unexpected outburst are achievable.

Honest limits of one modest station

  • Cannot determine orbits, radiants, or true velocities (need >=2-3 stations with timing/interferometry, or a real radar).
  • Absolute flux is hard: rate depends on your antenna pattern, noise floor, threshold, and the radiant's elevation vs your reflection geometry.
  • So compare RELATIVE rates from a CONSISTENT setup; note config in an observing log; and apply the IMO statistical-reduction method for activity work.
  • Aircraft & sporadic-E contamination must be filtered (your classifier helps).

Suggested first projects (achievable now -> better)

  • 1. Diurnal curve: run ~1 week continuously, plot counts vs local hour.
    • Reproduces the ~06h-max / ~18h-min sporadic rhythm. Great first result.
  • 2. Shower profile: capture Perseids (Aug 12-13); hourly counts vs time;
    • find your local peak; submit the counts to RMOB.
  • 3. Echo-duration histogram: underdense vs overdense; log-log duration slope.
  • 4. Daytime shower (Arietids, ~Jun): radio-only -- a genuine niche contribution.
  • Workflow: consistent setup + observing log (freq/gain/antenna/noise);
    • session_report.py per night; filter aircraft/E-skip before analysis.

Summary

  • One modest station yields real, contributable science: COUNTS, activity PROFILES, the DIURNAL curve, and echo-DURATION statistics.
  • Publish/contribute via RMOB, eMeteorNews, IMO campaigns, and the Radio Meteor Zoo.
  • It CANNOT do orbits/velocity/absolute flux alone -- be honest about that.
  • Better antenna (outside -> dipole -> Yagi) steadily unlocks more/better analyses.
  • Start with the diurnal-curve project; it confirms your chain and known physics.
  • Read the 3 PDFs in references/ first; the SARA 2015 one mirrors your setup.
